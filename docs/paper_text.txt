A hybrid actor–critic and BERT 
framework for intelligent course 
recommendation in IoT-aware 
e-learning systems
Xia Chunqin1,2 & Wu Peixi3
The necessity for recommendation models that can capture both semantic information and device-
mediated learner interactions has increased due to the rapid growth of IoT-aware e-learning 
environments. IoT-enhanced learning in this context refers to intelligent learning platforms that 
continuously create and log heterogeneous interaction data, including session dynamics, access 
patterns across linked devices, and engagement behaviors. This work introduces a coherent hybrid 
framework that combines an Actor–Critic reinforcement learning agent optimized using Proximal 
Policy Optimization (PPO) with BERT-based semantic encoding. By combining textual content with 
context-aware interaction logs gathered from intelligent learning platforms, the method creates richer 
learner representations. While a Mahalanobis distance module offers correlation-aware similarity 
cues to enhance resilience under sparse and high-dimensional data, these representations allow the 
Actor–Critic agent to constantly improve its recommendation policy. The usefulness of the suggested 
framework for IoT-aware intelligent e-learning systems is demonstrated by experiments conducted on 
three public MOOC datasets, which show steady improvements over robust baselines.
Keywords Recommender systems, Deep reinforcement learning, BERT-based feature extraction, 
Mahalanobis distance, Personalized learning, IoT-enabled education
Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs) have emerged as a transformative force in contemporary education 
by democratizing access to high-quality learning resources for learners across geographic, cultural, and 
socioeconomic boundaries1,2. Platforms such as Coursera, edX 3, and Udemy enable millions of individuals to 
participate in flexible and self-directed learning experiences. However, the rapid growth of available courses 
has led to substantial information overload, making it increasingly difficult for learners to identify educational 
content that aligns with their goals, competencies, and interests4.
Alongside this expansion, modern e-learning platforms have evolved into IoT-aware digital ecosystems 
in which learning activities are mediated through interconnected devices and online infrastructures. These 
environments continuously generate heterogeneous interaction signals, including session duration, access 
frequency, temporal engagement patterns, and device-mediated usage logs 5,6. Although such data do not 
necessarily originate from low-level physical sensors, they provide context-aware and behavior-rich information 
that is widely regarded as a practical proxy for IoT-enhanced educational environments. At the same time, 
the scale and diversity of these signals introduce significant challenges related to data heterogeneity, high 
dimensionality, sparsity, and privacy preservation7.
Traditional recommendation techniques, such as Collaborative Filtering (CF) and Content-Based Filtering 
(CBF), have long served as foundational approaches for personalization in educational systems8,9. Despite their 
effectiveness in static settings, these methods are limited in dynamic learning environments due to their reliance 
on fixed preference assumptions and explicit feedback signals, such as ratings or clicks. Consequently, they often 
suffer from data sparsity, cold-start issues, and limited adaptability to evolving learner behavior over time 10. 
These limitations become more pronounced in IoT-aware learning platforms, where diverse and continuously 
generated interaction signals require adaptive and sequential modeling strategies11.

To address these challenges, Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has gained increasing attention as a 
powerful framework for sequential decision-making in personalized recommendation systems12,13. By modeling 
the recommendation process as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), DRL-based approaches enable continuous 
interaction with learners and optimization of long-term engagement objectives. Within this paradigm, the 
Actor–Critic architecture has demonstrated strong performance by jointly learning policy and value functions, 
thereby achieving a balance between exploration and exploitation and improving training stability and sample 
efficiency14,15.
In parallel, advances in Transformer-based Natural Language Processing (NLP) have significantly enhanced 
semantic understanding of textual data. In particular, the Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers 
(BERT) model has shown remarkable capability in extracting contextualized semantic representations from 
unstructured text through bidirectional attention mechanisms 16. In educational recommendation scenarios, 
such semantic representations are especially valuable for modeling course descriptions, learning objectives, and 
textual feedback17. While Transformer-based models have been successfully applied to general recommendation 
tasks, their integration with reinforcement learning for adaptive course recommendation especially within IoT-
aware MOOC environments remains relatively underexplored18.
Despite their promise, IoT-aware e-learning environments raise additional concerns related to privacy, 
interpretability, and fairness. Continuous logging of learner interactions across connected platforms may expose 
sensitive behavioral patterns, necessitating compliance with data protection regulations such as the General Data 
Protection Regulation (GDPR)19. Moreover, ensuring transparency through Explainable Artificial Intelligence 
(XAI) mechanisms is essential for fostering trust and acceptance of intelligent recommendation systems in 
educational contexts20.
In response to these challenges, this study presents a hybrid Personalized Course Recommendation (PCR) 
framework grounded in Deep Reinforcement Learning. The proposed framework integrates BERT-based semantic 
representations with IoT-aware, device-mediated learner interaction features and an Actor–Critic architecture 
optimized using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). Unlike prior approaches that emphasize either semantic 
modeling or behavioral personalization in isolation, the framework unifies content understanding, contextual 
awareness, and adaptive policy learning within a single scalable architecture.
The proposed system is designed to address key challenges in intelligent course recommendation within IoT-
aware e-learning environments through the following objectives:
• To capture rich semantic relationships among educational resources using BERT-based contextual embed -
dings.
• To leverage IoT-aware, device-mediated learner interaction signals for adaptive and sequential decision-mak-
ing.
• To enhance learner engagement and satisfaction through continuous policy refinement using reinforcement 
learning.
• To incorporate privacy-awareness and interpretability considerations into the recommendation process.
To further improve robustness under sparse and high-dimensional interaction data, the framework integrates a 
Mahalanobis distance–based similarity module that explicitly models feature correlations and mitigates sparsity 
in user–course interactions.
The main contributions of this study can be summarized as follows:
• A cohesive deep reinforcement learning framework that integrates BERT-based semantic representation with 
IoT-aware interaction modeling and Actor–Critic policy learning.
• A correlation-aware similarity mechanism based on Mahalanobis distance, designed to improve robustness in 
high-dimensional educational data spaces.
• A transparent and privacy-conscious personalization strategy tailored for smart and IoT-aware e-learning 
environments.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section   1 reviews related work on deep learning– and 
reinforcement learning–based recommendation systems. Section  2 presents the proposed system model and 
training methodology. Section  3 reports the experimental setup, results, and comparative analysis. Section  4 
concludes the study and outlines potential directions for future research.
Related works
Intelligent recommendation in e-learning has gradually evolved from static, rule-based approaches toward adaptive 
and data-driven frameworks that leverage advances in deep learning, reinforcement learning, and graph-based 
modeling. Early course recommendation systems primarily relied on Collaborative Filtering (CF) and Content-
Based Filtering (CBF) techniques, which infer learner preferences from similarities in historical interactions 
and content metadata20,21. Although widely adopted, these methods face persistent challenges, including data 
sparsity, cold-start problems, and limited scalability in scenarios with sparse user–item interactions22. Moreover, 
their static design restricts their ability to adapt to the dynamic and continuously evolving learning preferences 
typical of large-scale MOOC platforms and IoT-aware digital learning environments23.
To mitigate these limitations, hybrid and deep neural architectures have been proposed to jointly 
model semantic and behavioral information. Models such as DeepFM and Neural Collaborative Filtering 
capture nonlinear relationships between learner attributes and educational resources, leading to improved 
recommendation accuracy24. However, these approaches are predominantly trained in an offline manner and

lack mechanisms for sequential policy adaptation based on real-time learner feedback, which constrains their 
effectiveness in dynamic learning settings25.
More recently, Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has emerged as a prominent paradigm for adaptive 
and sequential recommendation. DRL-based recommender systems formulate the recommendation task as a 
sequential decision-making problem, enabling long-term optimization of learner engagement through repeated 
interactions26,27. Within this paradigm, Actor–Critic architectures have demonstrated strong performance by 
jointly learning policy and value functions, thereby improving training stability, convergence behavior, and 
generalization compared to value-based reinforcement learning methods.
Graph-based and heterogeneous network models have further enhanced recommendation performance 
by explicitly modeling complex relationships among learners, courses, and auxiliary entities. A representative 
example is the heterogeneous graph-enhanced recommendation framework proposed in 28, which integrates 
graph attention mechanisms with deep reinforcement learning to capture latent relational dependencies among 
courses, concepts, and instructors. In parallel, Transformer-based models such as BERT4Rec have significantly 
advanced semantic modeling in recommendation systems by leveraging bidirectional attention to encode 
contextual dependencies within user–item interaction sequences29. Extending this direction, a knowledge-aware 
sequence modeling approach introduced in30 combines course knowledge graphs with deep sequence encoders, 
including BiLSTM, CNN, and MLP components, to jointly represent structural and temporal dependencies, 
achieving notable improvements in NDCG and Recall on benchmark MOOC datasets.
Session-based and heterogeneous graph neural networks have also gained prominence for modeling multi-
relational learner–course interactions. The SR-HetGNN framework presented in 31 constructs heterogeneous 
graphs composed of learners, sessions, and educational items, effectively capturing both short-term and long-
term user preferences from interaction logs. Similarly, a clustering-driven deep learning approach proposed in32 
integrates BiLSTM and MLP architectures to group learners and courses based on behavioral similarity, thereby 
alleviating sparsity and cold-start issues while maintaining scalability across large educational datasets.
Hybrid architectures combining deep learning and graph-based reasoning have been explored to further 
enhance recommendation robustness. In33, a CNN–graph hybrid model employs convolutional neural networks 
to extract student performance features and graph-based link prediction to uncover latent behavioral relationships. 
This integration improves recommendation accuracy and mitigates cold-start effects, outperforming several 
baseline approaches in terms of precision and F1-score.
Beyond predictive accuracy, recent studies have increasingly emphasized the importance of privacy 
preservation and interpretability in intelligent e-learning recommendation systems, particularly within IoT-
aware learning environments where learner interactions are continuously logged across connected platforms. 
Privacy-preserving paradigms such as Federated Learning and Differential Privacy have been adopted to 
protect distributed learner data during model training 34. In addition, Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) 
frameworks have been investigated to enhance transparency, accountability, and user trust in recommendation 
outcomes35.

data characteristics, strengths, and limitations.
This comparison reveals several open research gaps. First, most existing approaches address personalization 
from a single perspective semantic, behavioral, or structural without offering an integrated adaptive framework 
that jointly models all dimensions. Second, although Transformer-based models and reinforcement learning 
have shown strong performance independently, their combined application for adaptive course recommendation 
in IoT-aware MOOC environments remains limited. Third, correlation-aware similarity modeling and the 
integration of privacy and interpretability considerations directly within reinforcement learning–based 
recommendation pipelines are still underexplored.
Motivated by these gaps, this study proposes a hybrid BERT–Actor–Critic framework augmented with a 
Mahalanobis distance–based similarity mechanism. By unifying semantic representation, sequential decision-
making, and correlation-aware interaction modeling within a scalable and privacy-conscious architecture, the 
proposed approach directly addresses the limitations identified in prior work and establishes the foundation for 
the system model described in the subsequent section.

Datasets
The proposed approach utilizes three comprehensive datasets MOOCCube 36, edX, and NTHU MOOCs 37 to 
train and assess the model. These datasets offer varied course and user information, encompassing textual 
components (e.g., course titles, descriptions, tags) for semantic analysis and behavioral characteristics (e.g., 
interaction histories, ratings, engagement metrics) for user profiling. The datasets allow the system to identify 
intricate patterns, enhancing feature extraction and state representation for tailored recommendations. Table 2 
delineates the principal attributes of these datasets.
MOOCCube dataset
The MOOCCube dataset, obtained from XuetangX, includes 706 courses, 55,203 users, and 1,200,000 
interactions. The dataset comprises course metadata, including titles, descriptions, and tags, optimized for 
BERT-based semantic feature extraction, with user interactions such as ratings (1–5), clicks, and video views. 
These variables facilitate behavioral modeling and clustering by Mahalanobis Distance, hence improving 
recommendation relevance for various learner profiles.
edX dataset
The edX dataset, sourced from MITx and HarvardX courses, comprises 450 courses and 38,500 users, totaling 
850,000 interactions. It offers demographic data (e.g., age, gender, educational qualifications) and engagement 
metrics (e.g., ratings, difficulty levels: beginner, intermediate, advanced). These features provide thorough user 
profiling and enhance scalability and customisation, rendering the dataset essential to the recommendation 
framework.
NTHU MOOCs dataset
The NTHU MOOCs dataset, supplied by National Tsing Hua University, comprises 320 courses, 12,800 users, 
and 420,000 interactions. It encompasses behavioral attributes including interaction durations (0–20 h, mean: 
3.2, standard deviation: 1.5), course classifications (e.g., Computer Science, Humanities), and user actions. This 
dataset augments MOOCCube and edX by improving the system’s capacity to simulate changing user preferences 
in IoT-enabled contexts.
Dataset integration and rationale
The amalgamation of the three datasets MOOCCube, edX, and NTHU MOOCs guarantees that the proposed 
approach generalizes proficiently across diverse e-learning ecosystems. MOOCCube offers extensive behavioral 
and textual diversity, edX delivers organized and demographically enhanced interactions, and NTHU MOOCs 
furnish contextual behavioral indicators that signify student involvement intensity. The integration of these 
datasets enhances cross-domain resilience and reduces dataset-specific biases, allowing the reinforcement 
learning model to adjust to diverse educational contexts and learner demographics.
Preprocessing
The preprocessing phase is an essential initial step in the proposed DRL-based recommender system, aimed at 
improving the quality and consistency of input data from the MOOCCube, edX, and NTHU MOOCs datasets. 
This phase converts unrefined input into a structured format appropriate for further feature extraction and model 
training, tackling challenges such as absent values, scale inconsistencies, and textual noise. The pretreatment 
pipeline consists of three fundamental sub-processes: data cleaning, normalization, and text preparation, each 
utilizing stringent mathematical formulations to guarantee robustness and compatibility with the BERT and 
Mahalanobis Distance modules.

Data synchronization and feature alignment
Due to the datasets originating from several platforms, temporal and structural discrepancies may occur during 
integration. Timestamps are transformed to a uniform UTC format, and course identities are normalized 
using hash-based mapping. Feature alignment is accomplished through the application of schema unification 
techniques that provide uniform naming conventions and data types across all datasets. This preprocessing step 
ensures that input features maintain semantic consistency for both the BERT encoder and the reinforcement 
learning components.

IoT-aware interaction feature
In the proposed framework, IoT-aware interaction features are derived from device-mediated and platform-
level interaction logs collected from smart learning platforms. The public datasets employed in this study 
MOOCCube, edX, and NTHU MOOCs primarily contain standard web and mobile application logs that 
capture learners’ interactions within digital learning environments. These logs include session duration, access 
frequency, temporal engagement patterns, click behavior, and other context-aware usage indicators.
Although the utilized datasets are based on platform-level interaction traces, such data are widely regarded 
as practical and realistic representations of IoT-aware e-learning environments. In large-scale online education 
systems, learning activities are mediated through interconnected devices and digital infrastructures, where 
interaction logs serve as the primary source of behavioral and contextual information.
Accordingly, all IoT-aware features in this work are explicitly defined as device-mediated learner interaction 
signals generated through smart educational platforms. These features are normalized using Min–Max scaling 
and aggregated into a compact learner-context representation. The resulting representation is subsequently 
integrated with BERT-based semantic embeddings to form the final state vector used by the reinforcement 
learning agent. This formulation ensures conceptual consistency between the adopted IoT-aware definition, the 
employed data sources, and the proposed learning framework.

State representation
The State Representation module, illustrated in Fig.  1, produces a 778-dimensional vector for the Actor-Critic 
model. This vector integrates user features (10-dimensional) that include interaction histories and scores 
from MOOCCube, edX, and NTHU MOOCs, course embeddings (768-dimensional) serving as semantic 
representations from BERT, and clustering features obtained through Mahalanobis Distance clustering. 
Collectively, these components encapsulate user preferences and course interrelations, thereby enabling precise 
suggestion generation.
Action space definition
The action space of the proposed framework comprises a discrete collection of available courses inside the 
dataset. Each action pertains to the suggestion of an individual course, whereas top-N recommendation lists 
are produced by choosing the actions with the highest probabilities from the output distribution of the Actor 
Network. This architecture streamlines policy learning and guarantees the interpretability of recommendations, 
while the implementation of probability-based ranking provides adaptability in customizing the quantity of 
suggestions according to user interaction frequency or session environment.
Actor-critic model
The Actor Network generates a probability distribution over the available courses, functioning as the policy 
network that determines the likelihood of proposing each course depending on the current state, with its 
architecture specified as follows: The input layer receives a 778-dimensional state vector, derived from the State 
Representation module, which incorporates user-specific features, course embeddings, and clustering data.
The hidden layers consist of two fully connected layers with 256 and 128 neurons, respectively, utilizing the 
Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation function to introduce non-linearity and alleviate the vanishing gradient

The softmax output generates a probability distribution across the course set, with each value indicating the 
likelihood of selecting a particular course, thereby aiding in the ranking of recommendations.
The Actor Network employs similarity scores derived from the Mahalanobis Distance module to assess 
the relational proximity between courses and user profiles, thereby enhancing its output. This facilitates the 
generation of top-5 or top-10 course recommendations, as illustrated in Fig. 2, ensuring that the suggestions are 
relevant and diverse, particularly in addressing cold-start scenarios and sparse data challenges.

The Critic assesses the value function V(s), which denotes the anticipated cumulative reward linked to a specific 
states, functioning as an essential evaluative element that directs the Actor Network’s policy optimization within

Correlation-aware similarity modeling using mahalanobis distance
To model similarity relationships among learners and courses in a high-dimensional latent feature space, a 
Mahalanobis distance–based similarity module is employed. This module, illustrated in Fig. 1, plays a dual role 
by providing correlation-aware similarity scores and by supporting clustering-based feature augmentation for 
the proposed DRL-based recommendation framework.

Unlike conventional similarity measures such as Euclidean distance or cosine similarity, Mahalanobis

semantic embeddings derived from BERT with device-mediated learner interaction features. These heterogeneous 
components exhibit non-uniform variance and cross-dimensional correlations due to semantic dependencies in 
textual representations and co-occurring behavioral patterns in interaction logs.

Reward function
The reward function, illustrated in Fig.  2, acts as a quantitative metric to evaluate user interactions within the 
proposed DRL-based recommender system, technically described as:
 R = w1 · Completion + w2 · Quiz + w3 · Engagement (7)
The weights are designated as w1=0.4, w2=0.4, and w3=0.2, indicating the relative significance of each 
component in formulating the reinforcement learning target. The Completion Rate quantifies the percentage of 
a course finished by the user, indicating overall engagement and satisfaction (weight w1=0.4); the Quiz Score 
assesses the user’ s performance in evaluations, revealing insights into comprehension and learning outcomes 
(weight w2=0.4); and the Engagement Time measures the duration devoted to course materials, serving as a 
proxy for active participation and interest (weight w3=0.2).
This weighted combination directs the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) method in enhancing the Actor-
Critic model’ s policy, ensuring that suggestions correspond with user preferences and learning advancement 
over time.
A sensitivity analysis was performed to optimize the configuration of the reward function by varying the 
weight w1 from 0.3 to 0.5, while proportionally adjusting w2 and w3 to ensure their sum equals 1.0. This analysis 
revealed that the current configuration ( w1 =0 .4,w 2 =0 .4,w 3 =0 .2) yields a balanced performance across 
completion, assessment, and engagement metrics. This ideal configuration was confirmed using grid search 
experiments that thoroughly examined the weight space to enhance suggestion relevance and user satisfaction, 
as demonstrated by increased policy stability and convergence throughout training.

Training with PPO
The Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) module, illustrated in Fig.  2, trains the Actor-Critic model via a 
systematic and iterative approach that improves the stability and efficiency of the reinforcement learning 
framework, thereby optimizing the performance of course recommendations within the proposed system. This 
approach has three essential steps: advantage computation, policy update, and value update, each facilitating the 
model’s effective adaptability to user interactions.
The advantage computation measures the comparative benefit of an activity against the average, expressed as:
 At = Rt + γV (st+1) − V (st) , (8)
where Rt signifies the immediate reward at time step t, V (st+1) and V (st) represent the value functions 
assessed by the Critic for the subsequent and current states, respectively, and γ =0 .99 functions as the discount 
factor that reconciles the impact of immediate and future rewards, thus stabilizing the learning trajectory. The 
policy update utilizes a truncated objective function to avert excessive policy modifications, articulated as:
 LCLIP (θ )= E[min(rt (θ ) At, clip (rt( θ ), 1−?, 1+?)At )] , (9)
where r_t(θ) is the probability ratio of the new policy relative to the old policy, At represents the advantage, and 
ε = 0.2 is the clipping value that limits the update size, so providing a conservative yet effective policy refinement 
that reduces the chance of performance deterioration. The value update reduces the Mean Squared Error (MSE) 
loss for the Critic, aligning the projected state value V(s) with the actual returns, hence improving the accuracy 
of the value function across future rounds. PPO’ s design facilitates stable and efficient learning by harmonizing 
exploration and exploitation, hence enhancing recommendation performance through superior convergence 
rates and flexibility to the evolving preferences of users in the MOOCCube, edX, and NTHU MOOCs datasets.
Model convergence and evaluation metrics
The convergence of the Actor–Critic model is assessed during training by essential performance indicators, 
including cumulative reward, average policy loss, and critic value inaccuracy. The learning process is deemed 
stable when these measurements attain steady-state oscillations throughout successive epochs.
To assess the recommender’s efficacy, standard metrics Precision@N, Recall@N, F1-Score, and Mean 
Reciprocal Rank (MRR) are calculated across all datasets. These metrics offer a quantitative assessment of 
recommendation precision and ranking efficacy. Furthermore, training stability is evaluated via variance analysis 
of episodic returns, validating constant policy enhancement across iterations.
Privacy and ethical considerations
Given that the system handles sensitive behavioral and IoT-derived learner data, safeguarding privacy is an 
essential design need.
User identities undergo anonymization in the preprocessing phase, and no personally identifiable information 
is retained within the reinforcement learning pipeline. The model’s architecture is compatible with federated 
learning extensions, facilitating decentralized training across many institutional nodes. Moreover, transparency 
is improved by explainable decision visualization, wherein attention weights and incentive contributions are 
comprehensible to both instructors and learners, so ensuring ethical adherence and cultivating confidence.
Experimental results and analysis
Experimental setup
This section delineates the experimental setup utilized to assess the proposed Deep Reinforcement Learning-
based Personalized Course Recommendation architecture. All studies were performed on a workstation using 
an NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU (24 GB), an Intel Core i9 processor, and 64 GB of RAM. The models were executed 
utilizing PyTorch 2.1 and the HuggingFace Transformers library.
The learning rate was established at 1e − 4, the batch size at 128, and the discount factor γ at 0.99. Proximal 
Policy Optimization (PPO) was utilized with ε = 0.2 for clipping and a learning rate scheduler featuring cosine 
annealing. Each model underwent training for 100 epochs, with early stopping implemented when the validation 
loss stabilized for 10 successive epochs.
The three datasets (MOOCCube, edX, and NTHU MOOCs) were randomly partitioned into training, 
validation, and test groups at a 70:15:15 ratio. Stratified sampling was utilized to maintain the distribution of 
essential characteristics, including course complexity and user interaction frequency. The validation subset was 
utilized for hyperparameter optimization and early termination, whereas the test subset was exclusively allocated 
for final assessment. This arrangement guaranteed equitable performance evaluation among all baseline models.
The BERT module utilized the bert-base-uncased pre-trained model from HuggingFace Transformers. In the 
fine-tuning process, only the final four transformer layers were unfrozen, while the preceding layers remained 
fixed to mitigate overfitting due to the limited textual data available for each course. The model had a hidden size 
of 768, a maximum sequence length of 128 tokens, a batch size of 32, and a learning rate of 2 × 10−5 utilizing a 
cosine annealing schedule.
The AdamW optimizer was employed with a weight decay of 0.01 and a dropout rate of 0.1. The [CLS] 
token embedding from the final transformer layer was utilized as the semantic representation for each course 
description, with tokenization conducted using WordPiece, incorporating lowercasing and truncation beyond 
128 tokens.

Quantitative results
Evaluation metrics
To thoroughly evaluate the efficacy of the proposed BERT–Actor–Critic–PPO framework, four conventional 
assessment metrics were utilized: Precision@10, Recall@10, F1-score, and Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR).
Precision@10 quantifies the ratio of pertinent courses within the top ten suggestions for each learner, serving 
as a metric for recommendation accuracy. Recall@10 measures the proportion of pertinent courses accurately 
retrieved within the top ten recommendations, hence indicating the system’s capacity to address a wider array 
of learner preferences.
The F1-score denotes the harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a balanced statistic that mitigates 
the influence of extreme values in either measure and acts as a more steady indicator of model dependability.
The Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) assesses ranking efficacy by calculating the average of the inverse positions 
of the first accurately recommended item for all learners. An elevated MRR indicates that pertinent courses are 
situated nearer to the apex of the suggestion list, enhancing user pleasure and engagement.
Collectively, these four metrics offer a thorough evaluation framework for assessing both the ranking quality 
and retrieval efficacy of the proposed reinforcement learning model, guaranteeing that enhancements encompass 
not only accuracy but also recommendation relevance, diversity, and robustness.

Discussion
Table 4 unequivocally demonstrates that each component significantly enhances the model’s overall performance.
The most significant decline transpires upon the removal of BERT embeddings, leading to a decrease of 
roughly 3.7% in Precision@10 and 4.3% in Recall@10, so affirming the critical importance of semantic feature 
extraction in elucidating contextual relation between learners and courses. In the absence of BERT, the model 
fails to represent content effectively, diminishing its capacity to generalize across diverse themes.
The lack of the Actor–Critic architecture results in a notable performance decrease of approximately 3%, 
underscoring its importance in the development of dynamic interaction strategies via ongoing feedback. Upon 
substitution with a more rudimentary DQN-based framework, the system demonstrates diminished convergence 
speed and reduced stability in reward trajectories.
The removal of PPO optimization results in a moderate decline (about 2.5%) in both recall and ranking 
precision, indicating that steady and clipped policy updates are essential for averting reward oscillation and 
overfitting during training.
The elimination of reward normalization results in a minor performance decline (about 1.5%), indicating the 
role of Mahalanobis-based scaling in stabilizing the reward gradient and facilitating equitable learning among 
users with varying engagement levels.
These findings confirm that the integration of BERT for semantic encoding, Actor–Critic for adaptive policy 
learning, PPO for stability, and reward normalization for equilibrium control produces the most precise, stable, 
and generalizable course recommendation framework. The collaboration among these elements allows the 
proposed approach to preserve interpretability while attaining consistent performance improvements across all 
evaluation measures.

Discussion and implications
The results from the previous experiments collectively illustrate the efficacy and resilience of the proposed 
BERT–Actor–Critic–PPO framework for tailored course recommendations in online learning settings.
The amalgamation of semantic representation learning via BERT with reinforcement learning techniques 
through Actor–Critic and PPO optimization has yielded consistent enhancements in recommendation accuracy 
and stability across many datasets.
The findings demonstrate that the integration of deep contextual embeddings with adaptive decision rules can 
proficiently tackle persistent obstacles, including data sparsity, cold-start problems, and behavioral ambiguity in 
learner-course interactions.
The theoretical superiority of the proposed model substantiates the significance of integrating content-aware 
and behavior-aware features within a cohesive architecture.
The use of semantic embeddings allows the model to discern nuanced links between course material and 
learner intent, while the reinforcement-based policy framework guarantees ongoing optimization informed by 
feedback signals.
This synergy connects static recommendation systems with dynamic, feedback-oriented decision-making 
frameworks, providing a more accurate representation of learner interactions with educational content across 
time.
The ramifications of these findings are especially pertinent for extensive MOOC platforms and e-learning 
environments.
The suggested model’s capacity to provide precise recommendations with minimal computing expense 
suggests its practical integration into real-world systems where scalability and response time are essential.
Furthermore, its stability and generalization ability across diverse datasets (MOOCCube, edX, and NTHU 
MOOCs) indicate adaptability to different instructional environments, languages, and learner demographics.
This adaptability is essential for sustaining engagement and customisation in international online education 
environments.
The results emphasize the significance of adaptive policy optimization in educational recommender systems.

Conclusion and future work
This study introduced a cohesive hybrid framework, BERT–Actor–Critic–PPO, for intelligent and adaptable 
course recommendations in online educational settings.
The model effectively integrates semantic representation learning using BERT with reinforcement learning-
based policy optimization, capturing both contextual and behavioral aspects of learner-course interactions.
Experimental assessments across three extensive benchmark datasets MOOCCube, edX, and NTHU 
MOOCs revealed that the proposed framework consistently surpasses baseline models including SR-HetGNN, 
HGCR, CNN–Graph Hybrid, and Clustering-Driven Deep Learning, attaining significant improvements in 
Precision@10, Recall@10, F1-score, and MRR.
The findings validate that the amalgamation of semantic embeddings, adaptive policy optimization, 
and reward normalization yields a harmonious architecture that attains elevated accuracy while preserving 
computing economy.
The ablation investigation confirmed the significance of each component, while the convergence analysis 
demonstrated more rapid and consistent learning performance compared to leading approaches.
Notwithstanding a marginal rise in training duration, the suggested framework continues to be 
computationally feasible and scalable, rendering it appropriate for practical MOOC platforms and extensive 
e-learning ecosystems.
The study highlights the importance of adaptive and feedback-driven customisation in contemporary 
educational recommender systems, in addition to its empirical success.
The suggested model’s capacity to dynamically modify recommendations in response to changing learner 
behavior signifies a transition from static recommendation frameworks to continuous, policy-driven adaptability.
This adaptability significantly influences the improvement of learner engagement, motivation, and long-term 
retention in digital learning contexts.

Furthermore, incorporating explainable AI (XAI) methods into the reinforcement learning process will

Emerging paradigms, such as multi-agent reinforcement learning and graph neural policy networks, possess

The suggested BERT–Actor–Critic–PPO model provides a robust framework for enhancing individualized 
education by combining deep semantic comprehension with adaptive decision-making.
Its robust generalization, speed, and adaptive learning characteristics establish it as a potential method for 
developing the next generation of intelligent, scalable, and context-aware recommendation systems in online 
education.
